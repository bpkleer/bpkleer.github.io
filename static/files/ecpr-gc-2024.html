Motivation

… and what we already know

Why dealing with discrimination?

  • democratic citizenship established in the early years, crucial for political participation (Deth et al., 2011; Easton & Dennis, 1969; Verba et al., 1995)

  • experiences of non-acceptance/ maltreatment affect youths’ future involvement profoundly (Flanagan et al., 2007)

  • the reality of discrimination presents a significant obstacle in nurturing political involvement (Arikan & Turkoglu, 2023; Flanagan et al., 2007; Just & Anderson, 2014)

  • discrimination could also lead to fight discrimination and engage in politics (Dawson, 1995)

These experiences shape one’s understanding of politics and avoidance of involvement could be the negative result

To what extent and how does discrimination experience affect the political involvement of young citizens?

  • Dawson1995: Possible marginalized or discriminated groups can be defined by immigration, race, and socio-economic background.
  • data is cases regarding the general law on anti-discrimination: disability, sex, ethnics, age, religion/world view, sexual identity

Empirical evidence

  • political interest: Takyar (2019), Oskooii (2016, 2020)
  • political participation:
    • Pilati (2017), Fleischmann et al. (2011), Oskooii (2020)
    • Bilodeau (2017)
  • civic engagement: Christophe et al. (2022), Ballard (2015)
  • evaluations of democracy: Spierings & Vermeulen (2023), Bilodeau (2017)
  • effect depends on form of discrimination: Oskooii (2016) (political vs. societal)
  • Bilodeau (2017) showed that minorities experiencing discrimination participated less in regional elections in Quebec (Canada).
  • Oskooii (2016) split discrimination experience into political (institutional) and societal (interpersonal) discrimination and showed that political discrimination yields positive effects on political participation, although societal discrimination discouraged citizens from political participation.

Data & Methods

What we used and how we did it

Data

  • Children of Immigrants Longitudinal Survey (CILS4EU, Kalter et al., 2016a, 2016b, 2017):    
  • collected 2010 to 2013
  • unique in its focus on youth, particularly children of immigrants
  • Given the link between minority status and marginalized socio-economic background ideal data set

Analytical approach

Building latent factor of discrimination experience: testing latent structure (see Oskooii, 2016: political vs. societal)

Testing path model from discrimination experience on political involvement

        a) direct on political interest

        b) possible mediations via political discussions & civic engagement

Assessing measurement invariance for gender and between countries

Wave 2

However, we see some divergent effects across countries. First, we needed to free the intercept of discrimination in schools since this differed widely across the four countries. Interestingly, we see that discrimination experience is lowest in Sweden and highest in Germany. Furthermore, we had to free the constraint of equal intercepts for political interest, indicating existing differences across countries in the level of political interest (highest in Germany, lowest in England).

We freed the intercept of political interest, indicating male adolescents indicating a close to two-times higher intercept, replicating the general finding on the gender gap in political interest (Fraile & Gomez, 2017; Fraile & Sánchez-Vítores, 2020; García-Albacete, 2013). Furthermore, we freed the mean of discrimination experienced by police or security guards, indicating young men showing a close to two times higher mean than young women.

Wave 3

indicating existing differences across countries in the levels of political interest and voluntary work, and particularly interesting, in the level of discrimination in schools. Particularly interesting, we acknowledge that discrimination experience is lowest in Sweden and highest in Germany.

The intercept for male respondents was more than two times higher than for female respondents for discrimination experienced by police/security. Regarding discrimination experienced in schools, the intercept for male respondents is only slightly higher than for females. As in wave 2, the differences in the intercept showed a two times higher intercept for male respondents.

What does this mean?

  1. youth of immigration and from lower socio-economic background experienced more discrimination

  2. Although discrimination itself is bad, experiencing it rather iniciates more involvement, especially via civic engagement.

  3. discrimination does not lead to a higher frequency of political discussions

  4. differences across countries and gender:

    1. countries: means of political interest and civic engagement differ
    2. gender: means on indicators of discrimination by police/security and in schools differ (also political interest)
  1. clearly, as suggested by others; no surprising results, but not good results
  2. for civic education: nurturing more involvement in civic organizations could also lead to less bad effects of discrimination
    1. civic organizations actually empower youth
  3. political discussion seen as involvement as well as interest
  4. measurement invariances:
    1. country & schools: lowest in Sweden and highest in Germany
    2. country & polint: highest in Germany, lowest in England
    3. gender & polint: two times higher for male
    4. gender & police/security: two times higher for male
    5. gender & schools: male only slightly higher
